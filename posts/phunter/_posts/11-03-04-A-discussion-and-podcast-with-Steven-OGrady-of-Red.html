I had a discussion with Steven O’Grady of RedMonk last week; we talked a lot about what it means to contribute to the Outercurve Foundation. We recorded the conversation, so you can listen to the full discussion or read the highlights below.

There are a variety of FOSS foundations. Some are sponsor-driven foundations such as openSUSE, OpenStack, and fedora, and some are community-driven foundations such as LibreOffice and Apache. A hybrid model has evolved which combines the attributes of the two types, and is best represented by foundations such as the Linux Foundation and Eclipse. Outercurve is much like the latter in that we’ve been evolving our governance and structure to merge the needs of sponsors and developers alike.

The Outercurve Foundation is vendor neutral, plus license and technology agnostic. We achieve this via our legal structure, namely our by-laws. These three features are unique, and they ensure a project is safe from commercial interests; everyone’s on a level playing field.

I think the feature that makes Outercurve most interesting is the gallery model, which is how we gather related projects together. Similar to a museum, our “works of art” (projects) are collected around a particular theme. No project is accepted into a gallery without careful thought as to how it relates to the overall gallery theme and how it complements the other projects already in the collection.

Any organization can sponsor a gallery to achieve their defined goals. Thus far, we have created galleries that focus on a specific platform (ASP.NET is a good example); foster projects in an industry sector (the Research Accelerators Gallery); or solve a technical problem like interoperability (the SII Gallery). The gallery model gives a sponsor the ability to develop a theme and choose which projects it accepts, which helps everyone reach their goals - increasing project committer activity, increasing a platform’s usefulness, and building community and common cause.
